Systems Medicine of COPD: Multi-‘OMICS based Molecular Sub-phenotyping of Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease using Network Modeling Approaches

COPD is an increasing global health problem of pandemic proportions, which is likely to be caused by a multitude of etiologies including environmental exposures, genetic predispositions and developmental factors. Molecular sub-phenotyping and mechanistic modeling of COPD will therefore be essential in order to develop relevant diagnostic and treatment options. The development of computational systems medicine approaches and the availability of large-scale multi-omics datasets has provided the means to elucidate systematical alterations in the complex etiology of COPD. The project combines state-of-the-art systems medicine methodology and multi-omics datasets, representing a paradigm shift in the study of COPD and obstructive airway diseases. Expected results include not only improved understanding of COPD on a systems level, but also an in-depth probing of the nature of the inflammatory process in the lung. Subsequently, the results from this study may lead to improved techniques for early diagnosis of COPD as well as novel therapeutic targets.

COPD represents an umbrella diagnosis, with a number of different etiologies arising from an intricate interaction of environmental and hereditary factors. Two major hypotheses will be investigated: 1) we hypothesize that bridging and integration of multi-molecular- level data with environmental and clinical factors will provide improved power for subclassification and identification of COPD pathophenotypes, and that these sub-phenotypes can be investigated as function modules in molecular networks.; 2) we hypothesize that comparative analysis of molecular networks from COPD, resilient “healthy” smokers, and never-smoker controls can lead to identification of resilience mediators potentially protecting ‘healthy’ at-risk smokers from developing disease

The primary objective is to identify module-based biomarkers by integration-analysis of multi-omics data using network biology approaches, with subsequent module-based sub-phenotyping. In systems medicine, the mechanism of disease is not only caused by independent subsets of genes/proteins/metabolites identifiable by traditional statistics, but also by their interactions, making it important to develop a system-level understanding of disease. Disease subnetworks within specific cell-types, as well as exosome-mediated regulation between immune- and structural cells will be investigated. Furthermore, a probabilistic Boolean Network (PBN) model for COPD will be inferred, providing a dynamic view of pathology and potential prediction of therapeutic targets.
